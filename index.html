About this tool

A search and browsing interface for OCR-transcribed Ottoman Turkish handwritten works. Page images are shown with their detected regions and lines; the transcriptions are searchable across one or all works.

The OCR model works on text regions in multi-line level. This solves the issue of line segmentation for Ottoman Turkish manuscripts with complext layout.

This is an experimentary work on the use of OCR for handwritten Ottoman Turkish documents. Thus, the OCR'd output may not be good enough.

How to use

  • Choose a work from the Work menu, then scroll the pages (or click a thumbnail / use ↑ ↓).
  • Search any character, word, or phrase. Use regex for patterns (e.g. قر.ن), and normalize to ignore ye/kef variants and ZWNJ. Separate several terms with / (e.g. variant₁ / variant₂) to search them together and compare them in ▦ Stats; each term can itself be a regex, and \/ matches a literal slash. Search covers both line-level and region-level transcriptions, so text the model only captured at the region level is still found (its region is highlighted).
  • ⛃ Filters is where you set the scope and narrow the corpus. Under Search & browse scope, tick Regions to limit which region types are searched/browsed (e.g. only footnotes) and Works to limit which works — leaving a section untouched means “all”. Under Metadata filters you can further narrow by year, genre, author, publisher, translator, or subject. The button shows how many works are in scope.
  • ❒ Browse steps through every instance of the selected region type(s) within the selected works, one at a time. Pick your Regions (and, if you like, Works) in ⛃ Filters, then click Browse: the first instance opens on the page and the sidebar lists them all with a cropped thumbnail and per-work counts. Move through them with ↑ / ↓, or click any to jump to it.
  • ⇄ Variants (shown when a cross-witness alignment is available) steps through only the lines/regions that have a matching variant in another witness, highlighting them in purple. Whenever you select a region or line that has a variant, a ⇄ View variant button appears in the transcription box; clicking it opens a panel with the counterpart's page image and transcription (matched lines lit), and an Open in this witness link to jump there.
  • Step through search matches with ↑ / ↓ (or Enter / Shift+Enter); matched lines are highlighted on the page.
  • Toggle the regions and lines overlays. Click a line to read its transcription; hover for a tooltip. You can click to both text regions and lines. There is always a transcription on region-level while line-level transcription sometimes misses.
  • The transcription box at the bottom-left scrolls (each line is kept on its own line — scroll sideways for long lines). Drag the grey bar above it up or down to give the transcription more or less room.
  • ▦ Stats opens on a corpus overview (works, pages, regions, lines and words, with a per-work breakdown) when nothing is searched. Once you search, it shows how often the term occurs over time and across works. To compare variants, put several terms in the search bar separated by / — each is plotted as its own line so you can see how they competed through time.
  • The on-screen Ottoman-Turkish keyboard opens automatically when you click the search bar and hides when you click away. Fit fits the page to the window.

Comparing witnesses (variants)

When two works are different witnesses of the same text — for example two manuscripts of the Dîvân-ı Bâkî — their lines are aligned in advance so their readings can be compared. Lines or regions that have a counterpart in another witness are marked; selecting one reveals a ⇄ View variant button that opens the other witness's page image and transcription (matched lines lit) in a side panel, with a link to jump there. ⇄ Variants mode steps through only these aligned regions, presenting them in right-to-left reading order — the right column of a page before the left. After you jump into a witness, a ← Back button (top-left of the page view) returns you to the exact line you were reading before.

How the pairs are computed. Alignment is calculated separately; the interface only loads its result. The unit of comparison is a single line — each region's transcription split on its line breaks. Only running text, headings and marginalia are aligned; page numbers, and catchwords are ignored.

Reading order. Poetry here is usually set in two columns: the right column often carries a poem's odd lines (1st, 3rd, 5th …) and the left column its even lines (2nd, 4th, 6th …) considering the beyt structure is used, read together as couplets. Each page's lines are first put back into true reading order — two side-by-side columns are interleaved right-to-left (right line 1, left line 1, right line 2, …), headings stay within the running text at their position, and marginalia are kept as a separate stream after the main text — so a witness is compared as it is read, not one whole column then the other.

Matching. The two ordered line sequences are aligned with an order-preserving global alignment (Needleman–Wunsch): each line is matched to at most one line in the other witness, the reading order is never crossed, and lines with no good counterpart are left as gaps. The score is a normalized character similarity (Python's difflib.SequenceMatcher ratio); for matching only, the text is normalized — ye/kef spellings and ZWNJ are unified and vowel marks / tatweel / punctuation are stripped — while the displayed text is left untouched. A pair is kept only when its similarity clears a threshold (currently 0.55), so noisy near-misses are dropped rather than forced.

Caveats. Coverage is partial: it depends on how much of each witness the OCR transcribed and on the layout detection — the two columns must be segmented correctly for the interleaving to be right, and where a manuscript re-orders its poems the order-preserving matcher can miss the transposed passages. Because everything is automated, there may be some mistakes.
